1. **No, the paper does not need to prove alpha to be publishable at EMNLP.** If the evidence is clean, "task design gates access to look-ahead memory" is already a valid NLP/evaluation contribution. The paper's center of gravity should be: benchmark/task formulation changes whether models answer from temporally illicit parametric memory or from article-grounded reasoning. That is methodologically important even if the low-leakage prompt is not, by itself, a tradable signal.

2. **If you add an alpha-validity argument, the bar should be modest and targeted.** For EMNLP, you do not need a production-grade backtest. What is enough is one credible demonstration that the "safe" decomposition is not vacuous: for example, a held-out directional prediction test, rank IC, or incremental predictive value when combined with a direct signal. My preference is the lightest defensible version: show that at least one low-leakage factor has non-zero out-of-sample association with future returns, or improves a downstream predictor when added to stronger but leak-prone features. A conceptual argument plus citations alone is weaker; a full trading backtest is unnecessary unless you want to sell the paper as finance-first.

3. **Yes, S3 is the strongest angle for an NLP venue.** S1 and S2 are economically plausible, but they drag you into an empirical finance burden: now reviewers can ask for stronger alpha validation, portfolio construction, and robustness across horizons. S3 lets you stay on firmer NLP ground: decomposition-first prompting may improve faithfulness by forcing the model to form intermediate text-grounded representations before prediction. That connects naturally to context-faithfulness / reciting-vs-reasoning work. The claim should be modest: not "CoT eliminates leakage," but "task decomposition can suppress outcome-memory shortcuts and induce more document-grounded behavior."

4. **Handle the sham-control critique by changing the comparison class.** Do not defend authority by saying "it is low leakage, therefore good." That invites the word-count objection. Instead say: the relevant contrast is not between any useless feature and direct prediction; it is between (i) decompositions that remain semantically tied to economically meaningful properties of the article and (ii) arbitrary nuisance probes. Editorially: word count is a negative control because it is not a plausible intermediate representation of news content; authority/novelty/etc. are candidate semantic decompositions motivated by prior text-and-finance literature. You only need to show they are substantively grounded, not individually sufficient for trading.

5. **The safer framing is:** leakage-contaminated features are unreliable even when they appear predictive. That is much easier to defend than "low-leakage features are predictive." The latter creates a positive-evidence burden you may not want. The former supports a strong methodological recommendation: evaluation should prefer task formulations whose apparent success is less confounded by memorized future outcomes. If you want one positive sentence, make it weak: low-leakage decompositions may still be useful as inputs, anchors, or reasoning scaffolds in a downstream system.
