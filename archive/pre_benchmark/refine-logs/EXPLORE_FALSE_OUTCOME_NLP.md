# False-Outcome Injection: NLP View

False-outcome CPT and counterfactual article edits are related but not redundant. CF article edits are an **inference-time intervention on visible evidence**: they ask whether a fixed model follows the article's changed semantics or falls back to latent event/outcome memory. False-outcome CPT is a **training-time intervention on parametric memory**: it asks whether changing what outcome the model learned causally changes later behavior. So they target the same broad memory-vs-evidence phenomenon, but not the same construct. CF edits test **context-faithfulness / evidence sensitivity under conflict**. False-outcome CPT tests **causal expressibility of implanted outcome memory** under different task designs.

If I could keep only one, I would keep **false-outcome CPT**. For the claim that **task design gates memory**, it gives stronger evidence. The memorized outcome is experimentally assigned, and can even be false. If `direct_prediction` follows the planted false reaction while `decomposed_authority` does not, then the same model contains the same injected trace but different tasks differentially elicit it. That is cleaner than CF edits, which are always vulnerable to edit artifacts, semantic awkwardness, or the alternative explanation that the model simply distrusts the edited article.

In the memorization literature, false-outcome CPT is closest to **counterfactual memorization plus canary insertion**. Like Secret Sharer, you insert synthetic content and test whether it reappears behaviorally. Like Zhang et al., the estimand is the behavioral difference induced by controlled exposure. It only resembles **data poisoning** superficially: the mechanism is poisoning-like, but the research use is causal probing, not adversarial corruption of a deployed model.

The main NLP risks are practical. Too much CPT can cause **local forgetting**, calibration drift, or broader capability degradation. Fabricated article-outcome pairs can introduce **distribution shift** if the fake reactions are stylistically odd, economically implausible, or too frequent. The model may also learn shortcuts from article identity, source template, or ticker co-occurrence rather than a genuine outcome trace. This is especially risky for direct-prediction prompts, where the model may become globally less article-faithful or more overconfident. Mitigations: low-dose CPT, matched `article-only` and `true-outcome` controls, style-matched fabricated outcomes, and held-out capability checks.

Evidence intrusion detection still adds value on top of false-outcome CPT. Outcome-following alone tells you **that** behavior moved; intrusion tells you **how**. In your prompt setup, the useful question is whether the model imports false post-publication content into its evidence field or explanations, for example via `unsupported_quote`, `unsupported_post_publication_claim`, or `explicit_realized_outcome_claim`. If direct prediction both follows and **cites** the false outcome while authority remains quote-faithful and avoids those intrusion buckets, that is stronger evidence that task design gates not just output labels, but memory retrieval into justification.
