## URGENT findings (if any)
- No published paper found in this sweep directly validates the exact FinMem-Bench 3-bloc decomposition `{repetition/surface reuse, prominence/attention, institutional stage/source provenance}` in financial NLP, news-corpus memorization benchmarking, or general LLM benchmark factor analysis.
- The strongest literature-backed refinement is a **4-bloc** stress test, not the cold-reader 3-bloc: `temporal exposure / cutoff overlap` should be separated from the other three blocs. This is supported indirectly but consistently by finance-LLM bias work that treats look-ahead bias as distinct from prominence/distraction and by broader benchmark-validity work that treats temporal leakage as its own mechanism.
- The strongest methodological counter-finding is that **correlation structure alone is not treated as construct validation** in the modern literature. The most relevant construct-validity papers argue for nomological networks, MTMM-style convergent/divergent checks, and measurement-error-aware models rather than bare factor collapse claims from a correlation matrix.

## Executive answer to the three primary questions

### Q1. Is our 3-bloc structure validated by published work?
- **No direct validation found.**
- What does exist is only partial adjacency:
- [Revealing the structure of language model capabilities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10062) (Burnell et al. 2023) uses Bayesian and frequentist factor analysis across 29 LLMs and 27 tasks and finds **three** latent factors, but they are `reasoning`, `comprehension`, and `core language modeling`, not anything like FinMem’s repetition/prominence/institutional-stage blocs.
- [Evaluating Evaluation Metrics: A Framework for Analyzing NLG Evaluation Metrics using Measurement Theory](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.676/) (Xiao et al. 2023) explicitly proposes factor analysis for construct validation, but only as one tool inside a broader MTMM-style measurement framework; it does not validate any specific 3-bloc decomposition for benchmark factors.
- [A general pattern in the construction of economic newsworthiness? Analyzing news factors in popular, quality, regional, and financial newspapers](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1464884917725989) (Boukes and Vliegenthart 2020) operationalizes economic-news factors including `continuity`, `eliteness`, `influence/relevance`, and outlet-type differences. This is the closest domain-adjacent conceptual analogue, but it is a journalism content-analysis study, not a latent-factor validation of benchmark factors.
- Bottom line: the literature gives conceptual ingredients for each bloc, but **not** a published empirical validation that these ingredients collapse into your exact three latent constructs.

### Q2. Does any published work propose a different decomposition?
- **Yes.**
- A less granular alternative exists:
- [Pressure Reveals Character: Behavioural Alignment Evaluation at Depth](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20813) (Petrova and Burden 2026) reports that factor analysis over six alignment categories yields a **single general factor**. This is not finance-specific, but it is a clear adjacent example of apparent category diversity collapsing to one latent construct.
- A differently granular alternative exists:
- [Revealing the structure of language model capabilities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10062) (Burnell et al. 2023) finds a **three-factor** decomposition, but with factors that are very different from FinMem’s. This shows that “three factors” is not itself validating; the content of the factors matters.
- A methodologically richer alternative exists:
- [Quantifying construct validity in large language model evaluations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15532) (Kearns 2026) argues that standard latent factor models and scaling laws each misidentify constructs, then proposes a **structured capabilities model** that separates scale, latent capability, and measurement error. This is not a fixed 2/3/4/5-bloc decomposition; it is a warning that simple factor decomposition can be the wrong model class.
- A domain-specific alternative decomposition is also implied by finance work:
- [Assessing Look-Ahead Bias in Stock Return Predictions Generated By GPT Sentiment Analysis](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17322) (Glasserman and Lin 2023) explicitly separates **look-ahead bias** from **distraction/company-general-knowledge bias**.
- [Evaluating LLMs in Finance Requires Explicit Bias Consideration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14233) (Kong et al. 2026) treats **look-ahead bias, survivorship bias, narrative bias, objective bias, and cost bias** as distinct structural validity problems.
- Assessment for FinMem-Bench:
- The best-supported alternative is not the one-factor or Burnell-style three-factor solution.
- The best-supported alternative for your shortlist is a **4-bloc analytic stress test**:
- `temporal exposure / cutoff overlap`
- `repetition / surface reuse`
- `prominence / entity prior knowledge`
- `institutional stage / source structure`
- This 4-bloc refinement fits FinMem better than the cold-reader 3-bloc because `Cutoff Exposure` has unusually strong, independent prior art and does not naturally belong inside the other three blocs.

### Q3. Does any published work challenge the construct-collapse framing?
- **Yes.**
- [Construct Validity in Psychological Tests](https://psychclassics.yorku.ca/Cronbach/construct.htm) (Cronbach and Meehl 1955) is the foundational challenge: construct validity is not a single coefficient, constructs are defined by a nomological network, and common-factor evidence alone is not sufficient to settle construct meaning.
- [Evaluating Evaluation Metrics: A Framework for Analyzing NLG Evaluation Metrics using Measurement Theory](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.676/) (Xiao et al. 2023) states explicitly that factor analysis can only inform whether there may be underlying constructs and roughly how many there are; **defining what those constructs are requires further theorization**. It also recommends MTMM-style convergent and divergent validity checks to detect method bias.
- [Establishing Construct Validity in LLM Capability Benchmarks Requires Nomological Networks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15121) (Freiesleben 2026) argues that benchmark construct validity in LLM research should be grounded in nomological networks rather than thin benchmark-score interpretations.
- [Quantifying construct validity in large language model evaluations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15532) (Kearns 2026) challenges simple latent-factor readings by showing that latent factor models often proxy model scale and scaling laws ignore measurement error.
- [Do These LLM Benchmarks Agree? Fixing Benchmark Evaluation with BenchBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.13696) (Perlitz et al. 2024) shows that benchmark correlation itself is highly unstable to reference benchmark, model subset, and thresholding choices; high agreement is therefore not clean evidence that two benchmarks measure the same construct.
- In the finance/news domain, [A general pattern in the construction of economic newsworthiness?](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1464884917725989) (Boukes and Vliegenthart 2020) challenges naive collapse in a different way: the co-occurrence of factors like continuity, eliteness, and outlet type reflects **news production routines and audience structure**, not necessarily invalid or redundant measurement.

## Assessment of FinMem-Bench's 3-bloc structure

### Is the 3-bloc structure defensible given the literature?
- Your verdict: **NEEDS REFINEMENT**

The literature does **not** give you a published empirical validation of the exact cold-reader 3-bloc story. What it gives you instead is a split result. First, adjacent literatures clearly justify the **components** of the cold-reader concern: duplication/surface reuse is a real family in memorization work; entity prominence and company-specific prior knowledge are real families in finance-LLM work; and source/lifecycle/news-production structure is a real family in economic-news analysis. Second, the construct-validity literature is explicit that this is still not enough to claim a validated latent decomposition. Cronbach and Meehl’s nomological argument, Xiao et al.’s MTMM-plus-factor-analysis framework, Freiesleben’s nomological-network paper, and Kearns’s structured-capabilities thesis all converge on the same warning: raw covariation is not construct validation.

The best literature-backed refinement is to separate **temporal exposure** from the cold-reader’s three blocs. Glasserman and Lin separate look-ahead bias from distraction/company-general-knowledge bias inside financial sentiment evaluation; Kong et al. treat look-ahead bias as one structural bias among several; and the broader temporal-contamination literature from prior sweeps already gives Cutoff Exposure unusually strong independent footing. That makes a pure 3-bloc treatment incomplete for FinMem’s actual shortlist, because one of the strongest factors in the benchmark is not naturally captured by repetition, prominence, or institutional stage. If you include case×model variables in the decomposition story, the most defensible literature-grounded summary is closer to **4 blocs** than to 3.

The practical conclusion is not “drop the 3 blocs.” It is “downgrade the 3 blocs from literature-validated latent structure to descriptive working hypothesis.” That is a meaningful difference. The literature supports using the 3-bloc map as a stress-test heuristic, but not as proof that the shortlist has been construct-decomposed in a validated way.

### Are there refinements to propose?
- **Temporal exposure should be separated as its own bloc in secondary analysis.**
- Rationale: [Assessing Look-Ahead Bias in Stock Return Predictions Generated By GPT Sentiment Analysis](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17322) and [Evaluating LLMs in Finance Requires Explicit Bias Consideration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14233) both treat temporal leakage as distinct from entity/general-knowledge bias.
- FinMem implication: the cold-reader 3-bloc structure is not the best fit once `Cutoff Exposure` is included.
- **Prominence may need an internal split between entity fame and market/investment relevance.**
- Rationale: Boukes and Vliegenthart separate `eliteness` from `influence/relevance`; Glasserman and Lin separate general knowledge of firms from textual sentiment; Nakagawa et al. explicitly measure company-specific bias.
- FinMem implication: `Entity Salience` and `Tradability Tier` should not be assumed to be the same prominence subconstruct even if they correlate.
- **Institutional stage and source provenance should remain linked but not collapsed into one unanalyzed bundle.**
- Rationale: the economic-news literature treats `continuity`, `eliteness`, `influence/relevance`, and outlet-type differences as partly distinct newsroom routines.
- FinMem implication: `Structured Event Type`, `Disclosure Regime`, `Event Phase`, and `Session Timing` may correlate because of finance-media ecology rather than because they are redundant measures of one latent variable.
- **Any latent reduction should be treated as descriptive robustness, not validity proof.**
- Rationale: Cronbach and Meehl 1955, Xiao et al. 2023, Freiesleben 2026, and Kearns 2026 all point this way.
