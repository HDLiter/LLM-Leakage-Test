# Literature Search: Scenario 2

Search date: 2026-04-05

## Scenario 2

Low-leakage factors as distribution stabilizers or regularizers when mixed with high-leakage factors.

Working interpretation for this search:

- Prefer papers where stable, invariant, causal, or leakage-resistant features are used to reduce dependence on brittle in-sample shortcuts.
- Include papers where the mechanism is framed as domain invariance, anchor regression, stable prediction, feature drift adaptation, shortcut suppression, or auxiliary-task regularization rather than literal "data leakage."
- Prefer papers that say some version of: a strong-looking predictor can be fragile under shift, while cleaner features or objectives improve out-of-sample persistence.

## Quick takeaways

The strongest Scenario 2 matches are not papers that literally say "mix leaked and clean factors," but papers that implement the same mechanism under different names:

1. Stable prediction papers explicitly try to suppress unstable correlations so performance survives unknown environments.
2. IRM / anchor-regression / information-bottleneck papers formalize the idea that invariant features should anchor prediction when spurious features vary.
3. Clinical ML gives unusually direct examples: process variables and measurement indicators often improve local fit but can damage transportability unless handled carefully.
4. Finance gives the temporal version of the same story: signals that look strong in-sample often decay after publication, after crowding, or when the news-return mapping changes.

The closest direct S2 papers overall are:

- Kuang et al. 2018, *Stable Prediction across Unknown Environments*
- Kuang et al. 2020, *Stable Prediction with Model Misspecification and Agnostic Distribution Shift*
- Rothenhausler et al. 2021, *Anchor Regression: Heterogeneous Data Meet Causality*
- Futoma et al. 2021, *The Blessing and Curse of Measurement Indicator Variables*
- Glasserman and Lin 2024, *Assessing Look-Ahead Bias in Stock Return Predictions Generated by GPT Sentiment Analysis*
- He et al. 2025, *Chronologically Consistent Large Language Models*

One clear gap after the search: I did not find a canonical paper that directly runs the exact experiment "take a contaminated feature, add a leakage-free feature, and show the clean feature regularizes OOS decay." The evidence is strong but scattered across domain generalization, clinical transportability, and finance alpha-decay literatures.

## A. Feature Robustness / Domain Adaptation

### Domain-Adversarial Training of Neural Networks

Authors: Yaroslav Ganin, Evgeniya Ustinova, Hana Ajakan, Pascal Germain, Hugo Larochelle, Francois Laviolette, Mario Marchand, Victor Lempitsky  
Year: 2016  
Venue: Journal of Machine Learning Research  
URL: https://jmlr.org/papers/v17/15-239.html

Summary: This paper learns features that are predictive for the main task while being explicitly non-predictive of domain identity. The adversarial objective reduces reliance on domain-specific artifacts and improves transfer across domains.

Relation to S2: This is a clean cross-domain precedent for treating stable features as a regularizer against brittle, domain-specific signal. In S2 terms, the low-leakage component is the part of the representation that survives when the environment changes.

### Stable Prediction across Unknown Environments

Authors: Kun Kuang, Peng Cui, Susan Athey, Ruoxuan Xiong, Bo Li  
Year: 2018  
Venue: ACM SIGKDD Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1145/3219819.3220082

Summary: The paper proposes stable prediction methods for settings where the future test environment is unknown and train-test distributions may differ. The core idea is to reduce dependence on environment-specific covariate correlations so the learned predictor is driven by relationships that are more likely to persist.

Relation to S2: This is one of the most direct abstract matches to your hypothesis. The whole point is that "cleaner" structure should temper in-sample fit enough to improve OOS stability under unseen shifts.

### Stable Prediction with Model Misspecification and Agnostic Distribution Shift

Authors: Kun Kuang, Ruoxuan Xiong, Peng Cui, Susan Athey, Bo Li  
Year: 2020  
Venue: Proceedings of the AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v34i04.5876

Summary: The authors argue that model misspecification plus agnostic distribution shift makes parameter estimates unstable because covariate correlations can inflate the contribution of the wrong variables. Their Decorrelated Weighting Regression explicitly reweights samples to decorrelate variables and produces more stable prediction under unknown test shifts.

Relation to S2: This is almost a formal S2 paper. It says unstable correlations can dominate naive fitting, and that a stabilization term can make prediction depend more on robust structure than on contaminated co-movement.

## B. Anchor / Invariant Features in DG, IRM, and Robust Learning

### Invariant Risk Minimization

Authors: Martin Arjovsky, Leon Bottou, Ishaan Gulrajani, David Lopez-Paz  
Year: 2019  
Venue: arXiv preprint  
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.02893

Summary: IRM proposes learning predictors that are simultaneously optimal across multiple environments, with the goal of isolating invariant mechanisms rather than spurious environment-specific correlations. The paper became a central reference for the idea that robustness comes from features whose predictive relationship is stable across shifts.

Relation to S2: S2 is basically IRM in feature-language form. High-leakage factors are the unstable correlations; low-leakage factors are the invariant mechanism that should reduce OOS decay.

### Anchor Regression: Heterogeneous Data Meet Causality

Authors: Dominik Rothenhausler, Nicolai Meinshausen, Peter Buhlmann, Jonas Peters  
Year: 2021  
Venue: Journal of the Royal Statistical Society Series B  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1111/rssb.12398

Summary: Anchor regression explicitly trades off ordinary least-squares fit against robustness to future distributional shifts generated by exogenous anchor variables. The paper proves predictive guarantees under shift interventions and shows empirically that the method improves replicability and protects against distributional changes.

Relation to S2: This is the strongest "anchor feature" citation by name and mechanism. It is very close to the idea that a clean anchor should regularize a stronger but less transportable predictor.

### Invariant Information Bottleneck for Domain Generalization

Authors: Bo Li, Yifei Shen, Yezhen Wang, Wenzhen Zhu, Colorado Reed, Dongsheng Li, Kurt Keutzer, Han Zhao  
Year: 2022  
Venue: Proceedings of the AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v36i7.20703

Summary: This paper extends IRM with an information-bottleneck objective designed to suppress pseudo-invariant features and geometric skews that can fool pure invariance objectives. The method keeps informative signal while explicitly compressing away representation components that are not robust.

Relation to S2: This is especially relevant because it is not "throw away everything unstable." It is closer to your mixed-factor story: keep useful prediction, but regularize it with an invariance constraint so the unstable part cannot dominate.

### Deep Stable Learning for Out-Of-Distribution Generalization

Authors: Xingxuan Zhang, Peng Cui, Renzhe Xu, Linjun Zhou, Yue He, Zheyan Shen  
Year: 2021  
Venue: IEEE/CVF Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition  
URL: https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2021/html/Zhang_Deep_Stable_Learning_for_Out-of-Distribution_Generalization_CVPR_2021_paper.html

Summary: The paper argues that deep models often rely on unstable correlations that do not survive distribution shift, and introduces a stable-learning approach to recover features tied to more persistent signal. The method improves OOD generalization by favoring representations that behave more like stable causes than unstable associations.

Relation to S2: This is a direct precedent for "clean features stabilize contaminated ones." The paper's entire logic is that you should bias learning toward features whose predictive role survives when the distribution moves.

## C. Mixing Contaminated and Clean Features

### Feature Contamination: Neural Networks Learn Uncorrelated Features and Fail to Generalize

Authors: Tianren Zhang, Chujie Zhao, Guanyu Chen, Yizhou Jiang, Feng Chen  
Year: 2024  
Venue: International Conference on Machine Learning  
URL: https://proceedings.mlr.press/v235/zhang24cj.html

Summary: This paper identifies a failure mode the authors call feature contamination: under distribution shift, neural networks can learn predictive features together with additional uncorrelated features, and the mixed representation can fail badly out of distribution. The paper is valuable because it isolates the mechanism by which adding the wrong feature content can poison an otherwise predictive representation.

Relation to S2: This is the clearest negative version of S2 I found. It says that mixing in the wrong features can destroy generalization, which is exactly why adding clean anchors or regularizers could help stabilize the overall predictor.

### Stable Learning via Sample Reweighting

Authors: Zheyan Shen, Peng Cui, Tong Zhang, Kun Kuang  
Year: 2020  
Venue: AAAI 2020 poster / arXiv preprint  
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.12580

Summary: The paper studies misspecified linear prediction under collinearity and argues that unstable covariate dependence inflates parameter error under train-test mismatch. Its proposed reweighting stage reduces collinearity in the design matrix so standard downstream estimators become more stable across distributed data.

Relation to S2: This is a direct stabilizer mechanism for mixed feature sets. If contaminated and clean features are tangled together, the reweighting step is an explicit way to make the cleaner structure dominate.

## D. Concept Drift Mitigation Through Feature Design

### A Survey on Feature Drift Adaptation: Definition, Benchmark, Challenges and Future Directions

Authors: Jean Paul Barddal, Heitor Murilo Gomes, Fabricio Enembreck, Bernhard Pfahringer  
Year: 2017  
Venue: Journal of Systems and Software  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jss.2016.07.005

Summary: This survey defines feature drift as the setting where features become or cease to be relevant over time and reviews methods that adapt the selected feature set accordingly. A core conclusion is that explicit feature-drift handling can improve accuracy while lowering computational burden in nonstationary environments.

Relation to S2: This is the temporal analogue of your scenario. A low-leakage factor is useful precisely because its relevance survives when other features decay or become environment-specific.

### Adaptive Quick Reduct for Feature Drift Detection

Authors: Alessio Ferone, Antonio Maratea  
Year: 2021  
Venue: Algorithms  
URL: https://doi.org/10.3390/a14020058

Summary: Ferone and Maratea propose an evolving reduct for data streams that dynamically removes redundant features and adds newly relevant ones as feature drift occurs. Their experiments on benchmark data with injected drifts show that active maintenance of the feature set helps preserve predictive performance.

Relation to S2: This paper treats robust feature design as an explicit response to temporal instability. In S2 terms, the model should keep the clean, currently stable component and stop leaning on features whose relevance is decaying.

### Feature-based Analyses of Concept Drift

Authors: Fabian Hinder, Valerie Vaquet, Barbara Hammer  
Year: 2024  
Venue: Neurocomputing  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neucom.2024.127968

Summary: This paper reframes concept drift analysis around features rather than only around model accuracy, defining feature-wise notions of drift that support interpretation and monitoring. It also shows that feature selection can improve drift detection and drift monitoring, especially in unsupervised settings.

Relation to S2: This is useful for operationalizing S2. It suggests treating unstable or leaky factors as drift-prone dimensions and explicitly monitoring them instead of assuming every high-alpha feature is equally durable.

## E. Regularization Through Auxiliary Objectives

### Multitask Learning

Authors: Rich Caruana  
Year: 1997  
Venue: Machine Learning  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1007379606734

Summary: Caruana's classic paper argues that related tasks can improve generalization by inducing shared representations and reducing overfitting to any single task's idiosyncrasies. The auxiliary task acts as a structural bias that favors solutions consistent across tasks.

Relation to S2: This is the cleanest classical citation for the idea that an auxiliary signal can regularize a stronger but more fragile main predictor. A low-leakage task or feature family can anchor the representation away from contaminated shortcuts.

### Supervised Autoencoders: Improving Generalization Performance with Unsupervised Regularizers

Authors: Lei Le, Andrew Patterson, Martha White  
Year: 2018  
Venue: NeurIPS 2018  
URL: https://papers.nips.cc/paper/7296-supervised-autoencoders-improving-generalization-performance-with-unsupervised-regularizers.pdf

Summary: The paper studies a supervised autoencoder that jointly predicts the label and reconstructs the input, and proves a uniform-stability result for the linear case. Empirically, the auxiliary reconstruction objective never harms performance and often improves generalization relative to the corresponding plain neural network.

Relation to S2: This is a strong methodological analogue. The reconstruction objective is not the target itself, but it stabilizes feature learning and reduces the chance that the model overfits to brittle predictive shortcuts.

## F. Leakage-Aware Feature Selection and Stability in Clinical ML

### A Framework for Understanding Label Leakage in Machine Learning for Health Care

Authors: Sharon E. Davis, Michael E. Matheny, Suresh Balu, Mark P. Sendak  
Year: 2023  
Venue: Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/jamia/ocad178

Summary: Davis et al. provide a taxonomy of label leakage in healthcare, including post-outcome proxies, treatment variables, and subtle temporal violations. The paper is important because it turns leakage from a vague warning into a feature-design and validation problem.

Relation to S2: This is one of the best citations for why low-leakage features should exist as a distinct design goal. It supports the basic premise that some apparently strong features are strong only because they smuggle outcome information.

### Generalization in Clinical Prediction Models: The Blessing and Curse of Measurement Indicator Variables

Authors: Joseph Futoma, Morgan Simons, Finale Doshi-Velez, Rishikesan Kamaleswaran  
Year: 2021  
Venue: Critical Care Explorations  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1097/CCE.0000000000000453

Summary: The paper compares objective clinical variables with practice-driven measurement-indicator variables when predicting vasopressor onset across hospitals. Models using both types perform best in-sample, but recency-of-measurement variables by themselves have poor external validity; the authors conclude that such practice-specific features can help if adjusted for during development and discarded at validation.

Relation to S2: This is one of the most direct S2 papers in the entire search. It explicitly shows a feature family that helps local fit but is dangerous for transportability unless the model is anchored back to cleaner clinical variables.

### Validation of Prediction Models: Examining Temporal and Geographic Stability of Baseline Risk and Estimated Covariate Effects

Authors: Peter C. Austin, David van Klaveren, Yvonne Vergouwe, Daan Nieboer, Douglas S. Lee, Ewout W. Steyerberg  
Year: 2017  
Venue: Diagnostic and Prognostic Research  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1186/s41512-017-0012-3

Summary: Austin et al. develop methods for assessing whether baseline risk and predictor effects remain stable across time and across hospitals. In their heart-failure case study, baseline risk varies meaningfully, but many predictor effects are relatively stable, and the authors explicitly recommend preferring variables with temporal and geographic stability when aiming for successful external validation.

Relation to S2: This is a very practical clinical version of S2. The recommendation is basically to retain covariates that are stable across environments because they are more likely to hold up out of sample.

### Effect of Data Leakage in Brain MRI Classification Using 2D Convolutional Neural Networks

Authors: Ekin Yagis, Selamawet Workalemahu Atnafu, Alba Garcia Seco de Herrera, Chiara Marzi, Riccardo Scheda, Marco Giannelli, Carlo Tessa, Luca Citi, Stefano Diciotti  
Year: 2021  
Venue: Scientific Reports  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-01681-w

Summary: This paper shows that slice-level splitting in brain MRI classification can create severe patient-level leakage and drastically inflate reported performance. It is a sharp empirical demonstration that strong-looking signals may be artifacts of contamination rather than medically meaningful predictors.

Relation to S2: This is the contrast case that motivates S2. If a feature is "good" only because of leakage, then adding cleaner patient-valid signal should be thought of as protection against exactly this kind of false strength.

### Detecting Shortcut Learning for Fair Medical AI Using Shortcut Testing

Authors: Alexander Brown, Nenad Tomasev, Jan Freyberg, Yuan Liu, Alan Karthikesalingam, Jessica Schrouff  
Year: 2023  
Venue: Nature Communications  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-023-39902-7

Summary: Brown et al. use multitask learning to test whether clinical models are exploiting shortcut features rather than clinically proper signal, with applications in radiology and dermatology. An important result is that shortcutting is measurable and can be separated from other sources of subgroup unfairness.

Relation to S2: This is relevant because it uses an auxiliary objective to diagnose and constrain brittle shortcut features. It supports the broader S2 idea that one can stabilize a predictor by forcing it to respect cleaner structure.

### Shortcut Learning in Medical AI Hinders Generalization: Method for Estimating AI Model Generalization Without External Data

Authors: Cathy Ong Ly, Balagopal Unnikrishnan, Tony Tadic, Tirth Patel, Joe Duhamel, Sonja Kandel, Yasbanoo Moayedi, Michael Brudno  
Year: 2024  
Venue: npj Digital Medicine  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-024-01118-4

Summary: Across 13 healthcare datasets, this paper finds that shortcut learning from hidden data-acquisition biases can overestimate external performance by roughly 20% on average. The authors propose a bias-corrected estimate of external accuracy by explicitly measuring shortcut dependence.

Relation to S2: This is strong applied evidence that spurious features create a false sense of robustness. It supports the view that low-leakage features are valuable not just ethically or causally, but because they are closer to true external performance.

## G. Financial Feature Decay / Alpha Decay

### Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?

Authors: R. David McLean, Jeffrey Pontiff  
Year: 2016  
Venue: The Journal of Finance  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.12365

Summary: McLean and Pontiff study 97 published cross-sectional return predictors and show that returns are materially lower out of sample and even lower after publication. They interpret the post-publication drop as evidence that some apparent alpha reflects data mining and that another part decays once investors learn and trade on the signal.

Relation to S2: This is the core finance reference for why strong in-sample signals often decay. It motivates using more stable, economically grounded, or cleaner features when long-run persistence matters more than backtest strength.

### When Do Systematic Strategies Decay?

Authors: Antoine Falck, Adam Rej, David Thesmar  
Year: 2022  
Venue: Quantitative Finance  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1080/14697688.2022.2098810

Summary: This paper studies heterogeneity in how and when systematic strategies decay rather than treating alpha erosion as uniform across signals. The central takeaway is that decay depends on signal characteristics and on the trading environment, which makes feature design part of the persistence problem rather than an afterthought.

Relation to S2: This is useful finance-side support for the idea that some features are structurally more durable than others. In S2 terms, cleaner and more economically anchored factors should be expected to decay less than brittle, easily crowded ones.

### All the News That's Fit to Reprint: Do Investors React to Stale Information?

Authors: Paul C. Tetlock  
Year: 2011  
Venue: Review of Financial Studies  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhq141

Summary: Tetlock studies whether investors react differently to genuinely new information versus recycled or stale news and finds that information freshness materially matters for market reactions. The paper is a foundational citation for novelty and stale-information effects in text-based return prediction.

Relation to S2: This is directly relevant if a "clean" text feature is freshness or novelty. It says part of signal durability comes from separating genuinely new information from repeated content that will not travel out of sample.

### Predicting Returns with Text Data

Authors: Zheng Tracy Ke, Bryan Kelly, Dacheng Xiu  
Year: 2019  
Venue: NBER Working Paper  
URL: https://www.nber.org/papers/w26186

Summary: Ke, Kelly, and Xiu learn return-predictive text factors from a large financial text corpus rather than collapsing text into a single sentiment score. The paper is important because it treats text as a source of latent factors whose economic content can be more durable and informative than coarse tone labels.

Relation to S2: This is one of the best finance citations for replacing a brittle direct predictor with a richer factor representation. It supports the idea that better feature design can improve both predictive content and persistence.

### Time Variation in the News-Returns Relationship

Authors: Paul Glasserman, Fulin Li, Harry Mamaysky  
Year: 2025  
Venue: Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis  
URL: https://doi.org/10.1017/S0022109023001369

Summary: This paper shows that the speed and magnitude of stock-price responses to news vary substantially over time with intermediary risk-bearing capacity, passive ownership, and news informativeness. It also finds that a long-short strategy based on news sentiment earns higher returns when conditioning on these interaction variables.

Relation to S2: This is a strong finance analogue of your mixed-factor hypothesis. A raw news signal is not stable on its own; conditioning variables act like anchors that tell you when the signal should be trusted and scaled.

### Assessing Look-Ahead Bias in Stock Return Predictions Generated by GPT Sentiment Analysis

Authors: Paul Glasserman, Caden Lin  
Year: 2024  
Venue: The Journal of Financial Data Science  
URL: https://doi.org/10.3905/jfds.2023.1.143

Summary: Glasserman and Lin revisit GPT-based stock prediction under stricter temporal controls designed to reduce training-set overlap and look-ahead bias. Their main result is that a meaningful share of apparent predictability in historical GPT-sentiment backtests can be explained by contamination rather than genuine real-time forecasting skill.

Relation to S2: This is the most direct finance citation for the "high leakage wins in sample, then dies out of sample" claim. It makes the case for cleaner, temporally valid features even if they initially look weaker.

### Chronologically Consistent Large Language Models

Authors: Songrun He, Steve Yang, Alex Chinco, Marina Halac  
Year: 2025  
Venue: arXiv preprint  
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.21206

Summary: This paper trains chronologically consistent language models that only see text available up to each historical date and then applies them to next-day stock-return prediction from financial news. The authors report performance comparable to a much larger unrestricted model, implying that temporally clean feature extraction can remain economically useful even after removing obvious look-ahead contamination.

Relation to S2: This is one of the strongest current finance papers for your stabilizer idea. It suggests that low-leakage text features may be more competitive than expected and can anchor a forecasting system without depending on contaminated historical knowledge.

## Bottom line

The best cross-domain support for Scenario 2 is:

1. Stable-learning papers say robust performance comes from suppressing unstable correlations, not from maximizing in-sample fit.
2. Clinical ML shows that practice-driven or leakage-prone variables often improve local accuracy but can hurt transportability unless models are anchored to cleaner features.
3. Finance shows the same mechanism through time: fragile alpha decays, while temporally valid and economically grounded features are more likely to persist.

If I had to defend S2 in one sentence after this search: the literature strongly supports the mechanism "cleaner features or auxiliary objectives regularize against unstable shortcuts," but the evidence is distributed across several literatures rather than packaged under one standard phrase.
