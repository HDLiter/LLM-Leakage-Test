## Highlights

- We identify look-ahead bias by querying the model to recall a specific information with no context.
- Look ahead bias is more severe for lower frequencies, indexes, and large models.
- When working with relatively small models at high frequencies look-ahead bias is insignificant.

## Abstract

Look-ahead bias in Large Language Models (LLMs) arises when information that would not have been available at the time of prediction is included in the training data and inflates prediction performance. This paper proposes a practical methodology to quantify look-ahead bias in financial applications. By prompting LLMs to retrieve historical stock returns without context, we construct a proxy to estimate memorization-driven predictability. We show that the bias varies predictably with data frequency, model size, and aggregation level: smaller models and finer data granularity exhibit negligible bias. Our results help researchers navigate the trade-off between statistical power and bias in LLMs.

## 1. Introduction

Applications of large language models (LLMs) in financial analysis have raised a critical methodological issue: the look-ahead bias (see, e.g., Sarkar and Vafa, 2024, Lopez-Lira et al., 2025). As commercial LLMs are trained on extensive datasets, they can "recall" information that would not have been available at the time of prediction. This recollection can result in biases and false positives that artificially inflate the back-testing performance of LLM-based portfolios or any other task that requires predicting an economic variable. 1 Several conservative methods address these concerns. They include restricting samples to data after the LLM training cutoff (see, e.g., Lopez-Lira and Tang, 2023, Pham and Cunningham, 2024, Ludwig et al., 2025), using time-stamped LLMs to exclude forward-looking information (see, e.g., Sarkar, 2024, He et al., 2025), and removing identifying details from the data (see, e.g., Glasserman and Lin, 2023, Engelberg et al., 2025).

While these approaches mitigate the look-ahead bias, they also reduce analytical quality by shortening evaluation periods, manipulating the data, or relying on smaller, weaker models. Financial practitioners thus encounter a bias-power trade-off. Advanced LLMs offer stronger predictive ability but risk bias, whereas smaller or restricted models avoid bias at the expense of performance and profitability. Correctly estimating the magnitude of the look-ahead bias could significantly ease this trade-off.

In this paper, we propose a straightforward methodology applicable to most economics-related problems. The method involves querying the LLM without context and measuring the accuracy of its predictions. We find significant variation in look-ahead bias. The bias decreases with higher data frequencies, lower-capacity models, and more granular data (e.g., individual stocks rather than indexes).

Since LLMs cannot be entirely separated from their training data, precisely measuring look-ahead bias is impossible. To address this issue, we construct our measure by prompting the model to retrieve the target variable (e.g., stock return on a specific date) without any context. We call our proxy "pure look-ahead bias", as any additional information beyond the specific firm and date might trigger more correct recalls by the LLM. By quantifying the magnitude of the pure look-ahead bias, researchers and practitioners can estimate the magnitude of the true look-ahead bias in an empirical exercise. Moreover, by systematically identifying and excluding easily recalled observations (i.e., with larger bias), one can use larger models to increase statistical power and improve the credibility of back-testing results with minimal data loss.

For our analysis, we use GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-mini, and GPT-4.1-nano to retrieve returns of the cross-section of US stocks and major equity indexes between 2018 and 2023, at daily, monthly, and annual frequencies. We construct portfolio strategies based on the returns retrieved by the LLM. Specifically, we implement a zero-dollar, equally-weighted strategy that takes long positions in stocks with positive retrieved returns and short positions in the remaining stocks. Then, we sequentially remove LLM-retrieved observations, starting from those closest to the actual returns, until the Sharpe ratio of the resulting long-short strategy is close to zero, thereby ensuring the elimination of any predictability attributable to the LLM.

We explore three dimensions: data frequency, model size, and aggregation level (i.e., single stocks vs. indexes). At the two extremes, the correlation between the LLM-retrieved returns and the actual ones is 100% for the yearly returns of the S&P500 using GPT-4.1, reaching the highest possible pure look-ahead bias, and 0% for individual stocks at the daily level using GPT-4.1-nano. These results are consistent with the basic intuition that the LLM is able to correctly recall the information from the training sample stored in its parameters, but that this ability is far from universal.

Our measure of pure look-ahead bias proxies the lower bound of the true bias introduced by any prompt, as including additional information beyond the firm's ticker and date can raise the accuracy of recall. Recent research suggests this effect is likely small (Glasserman and Lin, 2023). However, added context might still enhance memory extraction by the LLM. To evaluate this contamination, we conduct an additional experiment, incorporating the same-day market return into the context. As expected, the number of accurate recalls rises significantly. With GPT-4.1, the proportion of observations to exclude to eliminate any spurious Sharpe ratio grows from 3.5% in the baseline setting to 22% with the added context. Yet, not all models show strong contamination. For example, returns retrieved by GPT-4.1-nano remain uncorrelated with actual returns, suggesting that contamination effects depend on the size of the model.

## 2. Pure look-ahead bias in the cross section

Our analysis focuses on equity returns. We estimate the LLM's recall accuracy by providing only a ticker symbol and a date, and then asking it to report the corresponding stock return. This setup reflects what we define as "pure look-ahead bias", which is a proxy for the magnitude of the true look-ahead bias.

Based on Prompt 1, we construct a panel of returns retrieved by the LLM containing the cross-section of US stocks between February 2018 and December 2023. 2 3 This period remains within the model training period. We repeat the exercise to build three panels of returns at three frequencies: daily, monthly, and yearly.

We start by focusing on the pure look-ahead bias of GPT-4.1 at daily frequency. For each firm, we compute the correlation between the LLM-retrieved returns and the realized returns. The right panel reports the top 15 stocks with the highest correlations between LLM-retrieved and actual returns. Most stocks show a positive correlation, with an average of 10%, and several blue-chip stocks exceeding 40%. These preliminary results suggest that the pure look-ahead bias is substantial and that the LLM recalls a non-negligible number of observations from its training sample.

The results in Fig. 1 suggest a fairly high pure look-ahead bias even at daily frequency. However, this analysis bundled together LLM's recollection of time-series and cross-sectional variation. We isolate the magnitude of cross-sectional variation by building dollar-neutral long-short portfolios, as performance can only be explained by cross-sectional recollection ability. Hence, we construct such portfolios based on the recalled returns obtained with Prompt 1. For each frequency, the portfolio takes long positions in stocks with positive LLM-retrieved returns and short positions in stocks with negative returns. In subsequent analysis, we report the Sharpe ratios of these portfolios as an estimate of the pure look-ahead bias's magnitude in the cross-section. Additionally, we develop a measure of concentration, i.e., how few or many observations generate this spurious performance. To that end, we define q as the minimal percentage of observations we can remove from our panel to make the recalled Sharpe ratios insignificant. Formally, we estimate for each observation the Retrieved Absolute Error, R A E = | L L M r e t r i e v e d r e t u r n - A c t u a l r e t u r n | | A c t u a l r e t u r n |. Next, we iteratively exclude from our sample observations with the highest RAE in increments of 0.1% of the total sample. We continue removing 0.1% of the sample until the Sharpe ratio of the long-short portfolio falls below 0.2, indicating there is no meaningful cross-sectional predictability left in the LLM retrievals. 4 We define q as the percentage of observations removed.

## 5. Conclusion

This paper investigates look-ahead bias in financial analysis using LLMs. Our empirical findings reveal that this bias varies markedly with data frequency, granularity, and model size. Specifically, lower-frequency data and indexes exhibit pronounced bias, necessitating the exclusion of a larger share of observations. In contrast, higher-frequency, individual-stock data display minimal memorization effects. Smaller models consistently show reduced memorization, reflecting their limited parameter capacity. Notably, GPT-4.1-nano demonstrates negligible look-ahead bias across all configurations, even when market return information is included in the prompt.

These results indicate that reliable backtesting with LLMs is feasible given an appropriate setup and the use of our method to detect look-ahead bias. By offering empirical benchmarks and practical guidelines, this work supports more robust and credible financial analysis with advanced commercial LLMs. Future research could extend this methodology to other forecasting tasks or examine additional determinants of LLM memorization.
